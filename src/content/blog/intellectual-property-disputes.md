<p><b>Intellectual property</b> (<b>IP</b>) refers to creations of the <a title="Intellect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellect">intellect</a> for which a <a title="Monopoly" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly">monopoly</a> is assigned to designated owners by <a title="Law" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law">law</a>. Intellectual property rights (IPRs) are the protections granted to the creators of IP, and include <a title="Trademark" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trademark">trademarks</a>, <a title="Copyright" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright">copyright</a>, <a title="Patent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patent">patents</a>, <a title="Industrial design right" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_design_right">industrial design rights</a>, and in some jurisdictions <a title="Trade secret" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_secret">trade secrets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_property#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Artistic works including music and literature, as well as discoveries, inventions, words, phrases, symbols, and designs can all be protected as intellectual property.</p>
<p>While intellectual property law has evolved over centuries, it was not until the 19th century that the term <i>intellectual property</i> began to be used, and not until the late 20th century that it became commonplace in the majority of the world.</p>
<p>The organization subsequently relocated to Geneva in 1960, and was succeeded in 1967 with the establishment of the <a title="World Intellectual Property Organization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Intellectual_Property_Organization">World Intellectual Property Organization</a> (WIPO) by <a class="mw-redirect" title="Convention Establishing the World Intellectual Property Organization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_Establishing_the_World_Intellectual_Property_Organization">treaty</a> as an agency of the <a title="United Nations" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations">United Nations</a>. According to Lemley, it was only at this point that the term really began to be used in the United States (which had not been a party to the Berne Convention),  and it did not enter popular usage until passage of the <a class="mw-redirect" title="Bayh-Dole Act" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayh-Dole_Act">Bayh-Dole Act</a> in 1980.</p>
<p>&#8220;The history of patents does not begin with inventions, but rather with royal grants by <a class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth I" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I">Queen Elizabeth I</a> (1558–1603) for monopoly privileges&#8230; Approximately 200 years after the end of Elizabeth&#8217;s reign, however, a patent represents a legal <a class="mw-redirect" title="Right" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right">right</a> obtained by an inventor providing for exclusive control over the production and sale of his mechanical or scientific invention&#8230; [demonstrating] the evolution of patents from royal prerogative to common-law doctrine.&#8221;</p>
